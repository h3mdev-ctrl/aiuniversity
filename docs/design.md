## North Star

**Can the recipient get the right outcome from the skill pack — on their own machine — without days
of back-and-forth?** That is the definition of success. Teaching, rails, and verification are all just
*means* to that one outcome.

## Product philosophy: the pack is a teacher, not a cage

The clearest frame for the whole product (Andrew's): a pack behaves like a real teacher — it
**gives you the skill, ensures you can actually use it, then lets you go do your own thing.** The
rails are **training wheels, not a cage**. Their job is to get the recipient's Claude competent,
prove it (the exam), and then get out of the way. After PASS the recipient operates autonomously and
adapts the skill to its own work — the pack has made itself unnecessary for that skill. This is
stronger than "a script that runs forever": the goal is a *genuinely capable* recipient, not a
dependent one. **teach → verify → release → mentor.**

Release isn't the end of the relationship. Like following a great teacher (Karpathy, a trader you
track), a pack can **touch base and mentor over time** — pushing updated best practice as the author's
understanding improves, which recipients pull when they want the mentor's latest. This reframes
"keeping everyone up to date" from patching rot into *following a mentor whose guidance keeps getting
better* — a relationship, not a one-shot install. Versioning is the mechanism; mentorship is the felt
experience (and the retention hook).

## The mentorship dimension (what packs uniquely do)

Following a mentor has three levels, and the world only serves the first:
1. **Consume** their content (tweets, videos) → you *understand*. (What gbrain/Vault ingest does today.)
2. **Execute** their practice (a pack) → you become *able*, proven by the exam.
3. **Stay subscribed** → their guidance keeps upgrading yours over time.

Content transfers understanding; it does not transfer capability. You can read every Karpathy post and
still not have a working training loop. A pack closes that gap: it packages a mentor's *known-good
practice* so you can do it, get verified, and stay current when they improve it. The difference
between a Karpathy blog post and Karpathy setting up your training loop, checking your work, and
pinging you when he finds a better way.

This also answers the trust question: **reputation is the draw and the provenance at once** — you run
a pack from a mentor you trust for the same reason you weight their words over a random tweet.
Mechanics (first-class): named reputation-bearing author; "follow" → "your mentor pushed an update";
pull → re-teach/re-verify. A living channel, not a dead installer.

## Problem Statement

Downloading a powerful tool or skill and actually getting it working is a multi-day guessing game.
Sparking example: **gbrain** — downloaded from GitHub, no idea how to apply it to Windows, and worst
of all it "wasn't even activated properly" at first: it *looked* set up, it wasn't, and nothing told
anyone. A friend hit the same wall setting up a Claude butler: days, tokens, endless back-and-forth.

Root cause: **when the recipient's AI doesn't know what to do, it reasons from first principles and
invents a path — and improvising is where it drifts.** (True even in normal Claude sessions: no
concrete step → the model guesses → it wanders.) There is also no scoreboard, so a silently
half-broken state persists.

The product gives the recipient's AI **something concrete to follow instead of something to derive**,
plus a checkpoint after every step so "not even activated" becomes impossible. Larger aim: one
canonical, maintained source of **best practice** that keeps everyone current — starting with the
foundation that makes Claude itself useful for the long run.

## The core idea: a checkpointed runbook (rails), with three modes

A pack's spine is a **checkpointed runbook** — an ordered list of concrete steps. Each step:

1. **Exact instruction** — do *this*, verbatim, not "figure out how to do this."
2. **A check** — confirm it actually worked.
3. **A prescribed fix if the check fails** — the concrete next action, never "reason about it."

The agent cannot wander: after every step there's a checkpoint, and any failure routes to a concrete
action, not to improvisation. **Escape hatch (required):** if a prescribed fix *also* fails (check
still red after remediation), the runbook STOPS and surfaces "human needed at step N" — it must never
loop or let the agent improvise past a broken checkpoint, which would reintroduce the exact drift the
product exists to prevent. A teacher who can't unstick you says "let's get help," not "let me guess."

Three modes of the same spine:
- **Teach** = walk the steps out loud, explaining as you go (built-in tutorial, no YouTube).
- **Verify** = run the checks silently → PASS or the precise gap.
- **Remediate** = follow the prescribed fix, not free reasoning.

We never *test* comprehension — we **teach** it, then **confirm by reproduction** (the exam). If setup
is correct the exam scores 100%; a wrong answer is a **diagnostic** pointing at the broken step. The
exam tests the setup, not the intelligence.

### Checks must be behavioural, not presence-based (core rule)

The single most important check-design rule. A **presence check** ("`gbrain --version` responds",
"memory_doctor HEALTHY", "the file exists") proves a thing is *installed*. It does NOT prove the thing
is *used*. The real check is **behavioural**: does the capability actually change the outcome?

- gbrain: presence = `gbrain --version`; **behavioural = ask something only gbrain can answer, confirm
  the answer came from gbrain.**
- memory: presence = `memory_doctor` HEALTHY; **behavioural = a cold recall probe — in a *fresh*
  session, situation X surfaces the expected memory Y and changes the answer.** ("Reachable in
  principle" ≠ "recalled in practice" — a HEALTHY memory whose trigger phrasing doesn't match real
  moments is *functionally dark*, and the structural audit can't see it.)

Every layer's exam must include at least one behavioural check, not only presence checks. This is what
makes "did I set this up right?" mean *right in use*, not merely *present on disk*.

## Architecture: everything is a runbook; a module is a runbook a bigger runbook calls

The product's one clean principle. Packs compose:

- A **pack** is a checkpointed runbook.
- A pack can declare **module slots** — a step that says "run module X's runbook here."
- A **module** is itself a pack (runbook) that drops into a slot: independently authored, versioned,
  and teach/verify-able.

So "a pack can include packs." This is the one genuinely new structural piece and must be designed in
from the start (the `modules:` notion in `pack.yaml`), not bolted on.

## The hero pack: "Set up Claude properly the first time" (the umbrella)

The flagship pack is the **foundation** — everything that makes Claude durable and useful over months,
not just working once. Andrew has already built the reference implementation of every layer below on
his own machine; that is the known-good path this pack encodes.

The foundation runbook sets up **six layers**, each a step (or group) with a concrete check:

| Layer | What it is (from Andrew's own setup) | Concrete check |
|---|---|---|
| 0. Identity | Who you are / how Claude should talk to you (`context/me.md`, `work.md`, `user_profile`) | Ask "who am I and how should you talk to me?" → specific answer |
| 1. Memory that compounds | `memory/` + always-loaded `MEMORY.md`; a **resolver** (intent→memory routing) so memory is reachable; the reflex rule (fail 2× → write memory now); `memory_doctor` audit | Write a test memory, new session, confirm it's recalled at the right moment |
| 2. Constitution + routing | `CLAUDE.md` = rules/invariants; resolver/skill-routing = the org chart. Two files on purpose | CLAUDE.md states hard invariants + environment up front |
| 3. Guardrails (hooks) | Deterministic backstops so mistakes are caught by code (`win11_quirk_guard`, `credential_guard`) | Attempt the forbidden thing (read a `.env`) → hook blocks it |
| 4. Capabilities (module slots) | Skills + MCP servers set up **and verified live** — this is where gbrain/Obsidian modules slot in | Each module's own activation exam passes |
| 5. Durability | Backup (`backup-memory`), decision log w/ rationale, maintenance cadence (doctor, consolidate, re-probe stale) | A backup exists and ran recently |

Honest tradeoff: the foundation is **bigger and fuzzier** than any single tool install. But it
decomposes into per-layer checks, so it's a *chain of small verified gates* — the rails model doing
its job. Most authoring work, most valuable pack.

## First modules (slot into layer 4)

- **gbrain-on-Windows** — Andrew's own scar; known-good Windows path already owned; the purest
  "is it even live?" case. Worked example below.
- **Obsidian LLM wiki** — set up an LLM-queryable knowledge base; another setup-heavy,
  environment-specific "get it actually working" module.
- Later: correctness-exam packs (nsw-site-planning), fuzzy packs (butler) via the proctor.

### Worked example: the gbrain-on-Windows module (a runbook)

1. **Install** → check: `gbrain --version` responds → *on fail (Windows):* run this; if "command not
   found", PATH is missing X — add it exactly like this.
2. **Configure** → check: config valid → *on fail:* exact fields to write.
3. **Connect DB** → check: `gbrain doctor` connects → *on fail:* on Windows use the pooler URL form,
   not the direct host — like this.
4. **Register MCP** → check: gbrain's tools appear and respond → *on fail:* exact block to paste into
   `~/.claude.json`.
5. **Activation exam** → check: run a real query, get a real result → *on fail:* points at whichever
   earlier step silently didn't hold.

"Wasn't even activated properly" is impossible: steps 4–5 fail loudly, and every fix is concrete.

## Windows-first (deliberate wedge)

Most tools are documented for Mac/Linux and strand Windows users — exactly where Andrew and his
friends live, and where the gbrain pain came from. Andrew already owns the known-good Windows path.
Underserved corner + owned know-how = the right place to start.

## The Bet & the moat

Three moat plays — **(1) packs that reliably produce the right outcome [product]**, (2) the pack
*format* as an adopted standard, (3) the verification data flywheel. Chosen: **(1), product-first.**
The scarce, defensible asset is **the encoded known-good paths themselves** — the exact steps that
work on a real environment, written once by someone who walked them. That is the best-practice corpus;
defensible because it's effortful. No physical lock-in (unlike NVIDIA silicon); moat = corpus + trust
brand + maybe-later standard.

## Constraints

- Solo builder, friends-first. First users: friends new to Claude Code, mostly on Windows.
- Author on Windows 11 + Claude Code desktop; recipients any OS → runbook steps are per-OS.
- Trust cold-start: bootstrap where trust already exists (Andrew's own skills + friends).
  *Acknowledged open:* bootstrap, not yet a general trust mechanism — CEO review will press it.

## Premises (agreed)

1. Value is ~90% in the pack's rails+checks+teaching, ~10% in the marketplace. Build the format first.
2. Launch on **setup/activation packs** first (checkable "is it live"), on **Windows**, where pain +
   owned know-how concentrate. Correctness-exam and fuzzy packs come later.
3. Don't *test* comprehension — teach it, confirm by reproduction; the exam is a setup diagnostic.

## Approaches Considered

- **A — Hand-built runbook per skill (MVP):** fastest; skill #2 from scratch. S / Low.
- **B — Reusable pack format + one generic runner (CHOSEN):** fill-in-the-blank `pack.yaml` + one
  runner doing teach/verify/remediate **and recursion into modules**. Skill #2..#N = fill in the form.
  M / Med. Seeds a future standard for free.
- **C — Live proctor agent:** proctor-as-teacher (richer teach mode) + home for fuzzy packs. Phase-2.
  L / High.

## Recommended Approach

Approach **B**, sequencing = **foundation as umbrella, gbrain/Obsidian as its modules** (Andrew's
choice). Non-negotiable guardrail: design the umbrella skeleton + the module notion, then **thread the
real gbrain module through it end-to-end in the same week** — prove composition against reality before
perfecting the umbrella.

## What a pack physically is

```
packs/foundation/
  pack.yaml          # the umbrella runbook (6 layers; layer 4 declares module slots)
  ...
packs/gbrain-windows/
  pack.yaml          # a module runbook (slots into foundation layer 4)
  SKILL.md
  files/  fixtures/  lessons/
```

### `pack.yaml` — v1 fields

- `name`, `version` (versioning from day one → packs can be "kept up to date")
- `steps:` ordered; each step:
  - `instruction:` verbatim action (may be per-OS)
  - `check:` one of `exact`, `contains`, `regex`, `number_within(epsilon)`, `json_subset`,
    `command_succeeds`, `endpoint_reachable`
  - `fixture:` (correctness checks only) recorded response the check runs against
  - `on_fail:` concrete prescribed remediation (runner has a generic fallback if absent)
  - `teach:` optional narration reference
- `modules:` slots — a step may be "run module <name>'s runbook here" (the composition primitive)

**YAGNI guard:** design `pack.yaml` for exactly what the foundation umbrella + gbrain module need. Do
NOT pre-generalise for the hypothetical "standard."

## Distribution Plan

- **Registry** = GitHub repo of pack folders (the "official source").
- **Install** = copy a pack into the Claude skills dir; run `verify` (and `teach` on first use). One
  command name: **`verify`**.
- **CI/CD = phase-2, deferred** until the wedge is proven.

## Success Criteria

- A friend installs the foundation + gbrain module, runs `teach`, gets Claude **properly set up and
  gbrain live on Windows without asking Andrew**, then `verify` returns **PASS**; "silently not
  activated" is impossible.
- Time-to-working drops from days to one guided run.
- Adding the Obsidian module is **filling in a `pack.yaml`**, not writing new code.
- `verify` is deterministic: same pack + same install → same result every time.

## Security & trust surface (CEO review finding)

A pack is **executable instructions that run on the recipient's machine** — installs, config edits,
shell commands. That's the product's job, but it's also a real attack surface: a malicious pack is an
**arbitrary-command-execution vector**. Friends-first is safe (recipients trust Andrew, the sole
author). But the *marketplace* form — running runbooks downloaded from strangers — is RCE-by-design
unless gated. This makes the earlier "how does trust scale beyond the friend circle?" question a
concrete security requirement, not a branding one:
- **v1 (friends):** single trusted author; no gating beyond "only run packs from Andrew."
- **Before any open registry:** pack signing/provenance (packs signed by a known author), a "review
  the runbook before you run it" step, and/or sandboxed execution of unproven packs. Must precede
  third-party authorship. Deferred, but a hard gate on the marketplace, not optional.

## Deferred north-star capabilities (TODOS)

- **Authoring-by-demonstration** — instead of hand-writing a `pack.yaml`, a Claude watches the
  **author** (Andrew, or any pack creator) perform a successful setup **once, on their own machine**,
  and generates the runbook + checks + fixtures from that transcript. Watches the *author*, never the
  *recipient* — so there is no recipient-privacy concern even beyond the friend circle. This is the
  capability that makes the corpus survive tool churn: when a module rots, re-perform the setup once
  and regenerate, rather than hand-maintaining. Deferred to v2; v1 must prove the runner + one
  hand-authored pack first. (Directly attacks the content-velocity existential risk from CEO review.)
- **Two rot-rates (design invariant, from CEO review):** foundation layers = **timeless principles**
  (identity, compounding memory, guardrails, durability) — don't rot. Modules = **tool-specific
  instantiations** (gbrain 0.42's steps, this week's gstack) — rot fast, swappable. The valuable half
  of the corpus is the durable half; only the module layer needs churn maintenance. "Apply the bulk of
  the skills" survives tool churn because the principles carry.
- **Platform risk (named, not solved):** Anthropic could bake install-verification into Claude Code
  natively. The runner is therefore a commodity; the defensible moat is the curated corpus of
  known-good paths + Windows-first + community trust. Build accordingly.
